Aikido / Budō

Another path to the same summit

Change does not have to be the end of the Path. Sometimes it is the moment when budō stops being a training schedule and begins to concern the whole of life.

June 4, 2026

The hardest obstacles rarely look like a great wall. More often, they take the shape of ordinary change: the body does not recover from an injury as quickly as we hoped; work takes away the evenings; family requires presence; the dojo is no longer available; a relationship breaks down; a plan that gave direction for years suddenly no longer fits life.

At such a moment, it is easy to assume that the Path has been interrupted. If you can no longer train as you once did, attend with the same frequency, pursue the same goal, or keep the same rhythm, the thought appears: this is the end. But budō is sharper and more honest than our attachment to a comfortable version of practice. It does not only ask whether the path is still straight. It asks whether the person can still walk.

The saying that many paths lead to the same summit only makes sense if we remember what the summit is. It is not a particular route, rank, technique, or weekly rhythm. The summit is a more mature human being: less ruled by fear, less dependent on ego, more responsible for contact with reality and with another person. If that remains the direction, change does not have to mean leaving the Path. It can become another approach to the same summit.

Budō is not only a place or a technique

In “Zen i Budo”, Ōmori Sōgen Roshi reminds us that budō should not be understood only as a set of fighting techniques, a sport, or combative skill. It reaches deeper: it is a Path for the human being, not merely a method of effective victory. That distinction matters. Technique can be precise, the body strong, the reaction fast — and the person can still remain imprisoned by fear, ambition, the need to dominate, or an image of themselves.

If budō is only technique, adversity in life really does look like an external obstacle. Injury takes away technique. Lack of time takes away training. A change of place takes away the dojo. Then it is easy to say: if the conditions have disappeared, practice is over. But if budō is a Path for the human being, the material of practice is much broader. It includes the body, time, relationships, duties, fear, loss, anger, aging, changing plans, and everything that cannot be comfortably controlled.

This does not mean that the mat stops mattering. Quite the opposite. The dojo gives us a language in which these things can be seen without pretending. It teaches distance, contact, timing, center, breath, correction, and responsibility for the partner. But the real test begins when that language has to describe more than technique. If the principles work only between the opening bow and the closing bow, they have not yet become a Path. They have stayed in the room.

When the old path disappears

A person becomes attached not only to the goal, but also to the way they have been walking toward it. To the favorite rhythm of training. To a role in the group. To the body that used to react faster. To the teacher, the partners, the ambitions, and the image of how development should look. The problem begins when we confuse that route with the Path itself.

In technique, this becomes visible immediately. Nage knows the form and repeats it correctly, but uke no longer responds as before. The distance is different, the tempo is different, the contact is less convenient. If nage clings only to the memorized shape, they begin to fight the situation. If they understand the principle, they can change the entry, shorten the movement, wait a fraction of a second, or choose another line without betraying the meaning of the technique.

Life does the same thing, only less politely. It removes the familiar form and checks whether there is a principle underneath. Someone can no longer train four times a week, so they learn whether one class can have a quality that four distracted classes never had. Someone after an injury no longer takes ukemi as before, so they discover precision, breath, patience, and humility toward the body. Someone loses an old professional position and has to find out whether their stability came from the quality of their work, or only from the title printed on a business card.

The old path may disappear. That hurts, and there is no point smoothing it over. But the disappearance of a path does not always mean the disappearance of direction. Sometimes it means the end of one form of practice and the beginning of a harder, less impressive, more honest one.

Adversity as a partner, not a verdict

In the dojo, the partner is not an obstacle to performing the technique. They are the condition that allows the technique to be tested. A good uke does not need to block brutally. It is enough to stay alive: keep posture, not fall too early, not rescue nage's mistake, and offer honest contact. Then the movement reveals the truth about itself.

The adversities of daily life work in a similar way. Not because fate is a teacher with a hidden plan. That story becomes cheap very quickly. The point is simpler: a difficult situation reveals where we really are. Conflict shows whether we can listen when the other person does not confirm our view of the situation. Failure shows whether our dignity depended on the result. Illness shows how much pride we carried toward the body. A change of plans shows whether we were present on the Path, or only attached to the map.

This is practice without romanticizing suffering. Not every loss is a gift. Not every wound “had to happen.” Not every difficult place should be endured simply because it can be called a lesson. Sometimes the right practice is to rest, ask for help, set a boundary, leave a dangerous situation, or stop pretending that toughness can replace judgment. Budō is not about confusing stubbornness with courage.

But if adversity is already standing in front of us, we can treat it as honest contact. Not as a verdict. Not as proof that the Path is over. As a situation that requires an answer from center, not from panic.

Another path does not mean anything goes

The phrase “everyone has their own path” can be convenient. It can be used to justify laziness, escape from correction, lack of loyalty, chaos, or the abandonment of standards. Then we are no longer speaking about many paths to the same summit. We are speaking about circling on the slope and calling it freedom.

Budō requires a stricter criterion. The path may be different, but the direction has to be checked. Does this change teach greater responsibility, or does it only provide a comfortable excuse? Does it reduce the power of ego, or protect ego from correction? Does it deepen contact with people, or turn independence into indifference? Does it allow reality to be seen more clearly, or only build a pleasant story about one's own uniqueness?

On the mat, changing direction makes sense only if it still answers the situation. You cannot turn any way you like and claim it is a variation of technique. The partner, distance, axis, and timing will check the truth immediately. Life is similar, although the test takes longer. A path that leads toward greater honesty, calm, courage, and responsibility may look different from the plan of years ago. A path that leads toward resentment, comfort, and isolation is not a new path to the summit. It is a descent.

Fearlessness without pretending there is no fear

Writing about Zen and budō, Ōmori Sōgen also invokes the theme of fearlessness. In practice, this does not mean a person who fears nothing. That image is usually a pose or a lack of imagination. Mature fearlessness means, rather, that fear does not take the lead. It is seen, but it does not govern the movement.

Change often begins precisely with fear. What if I do not return to my former level of fitness? What if I lose my place in the group? What if my work, relationship, or life plan does not return to its previous shape? These questions do not need to be drowned out with slogans about strength. It is better to sit with them honestly, as one sits before training: straighten the back, settle the breath, and see what is actually in front of us.

Then fear can become information. It shows an attachment, an area of responsibility, a place that needs preparation, or a real danger that must not be ignored. The problem begins only when fear is given the right to make every decision. Then a person tightens their hands around the old path, even though it has already ended, and calls that loyalty. Sometimes it is not loyalty. It is fear of the next step.

The dojo without walls

The most important consequences of this practice appear outside the dojo. At work, a change of project, manager, or role can feel like an attack on identity. A person starts defending the old position instead of seeing the new distance and the new conditions for action. Budō reminds us: do not confuse your center with the place you occupied. If the center is real, you can change position without losing direction.

In family life and relationships, adversity often does not arrive as a dramatic enemy. It arrives as fatigue, the illness of someone close, duties no one sees, or a conversation that can no longer be won by argument. In the dojo, we learn that contact with a partner requires feeling, not force alone. Life works the same way. Sometimes the mature answer is less pressure, a calmer tone, more presence, and giving up the need for immediate victory.

Aging brings another lesson. A person who has built identity for years on physical ability can experience limitation as humiliation. But the body does not betray the Path. It shows a new stage. Less dynamic movement can mean more precision. Fewer spectacular throws can open a deeper understanding of contact, safety, and responsibility for younger practitioners. Not every loss of range of movement is a loss of practice. Sometimes it is an invitation to a quieter practice.

In professional failure, the collapse of a plan, or a break from training, the same principle applies: the point is not to pretend that nothing happened. Something did happen. The path has turned, or perhaps it has broken off underfoot. But a person can still check the breath, see the facts, separate loss from wounded ego, ask for support, take responsibility, and make the next honest step.

This is budō in daily life. Not impressive philosophy and not soft consolation. A practice in which change stops being automatically treated as the end and becomes a test of direction. If the summit is a human being who is more present, courageous, and responsible, then many paths really can lead there. But each of them must pass the same test: are you walking with greater honesty, or merely escaping from a place that stopped being comfortable?

The Path does not promise that the ground under your feet will not change. It promises something less pleasant and far more valuable: every change can become a place of practice if the person does not abandon direction. Sometimes the Path does not end. Only the route we confused with the Path comes to an end.
