Five principles behind every technique

No matter the technique name, the foundation stays the same: entry, balance, direction, contact, and closure.

April 11, 2026

In dojo practice, it is easy to get lost in labels: ikkyo, shihonage, iriminage, kotegaeshi. Techniques look different, but execution quality always rests on the same principles. Practitioners who understand them learn faster. Practitioners who ignore them collect variations without real progress.

These principles are not theory for post-training discussion. They are operational criteria that immediately show whether a technique worked because it was truly built well, or because it was somehow forced through. Good dojos return to them constantly at every level.

1. Entry before force

If entry is late or shallow, the rest of the technique becomes damage control. First principle: position yourself so the technique starts from positional advantage, not from pushing. Ma-ai and timing matter more than tense shoulders.

2. Break balance, do not fight the person

Technique works when you take away stable alignment and guide structure. When you try to "win with strength," signs are obvious: movement gets heavy, elbows drift away from center, breath breaks. Kuzushi should be precise, not brutal.

3. Direction and continuity of movement

Every technique has a direction of guidance. If you keep changing vectors or stopping, the partner regains control. Third principle: from entry to finish, movement should carry one intent, without random pauses and without patched endings.

4. Contact and information reading

A good tori does not guess blindly. They read tension, resistance, rhythm change, and adjust in real time. Fourth principle: partner work is not mannequin work. Hand contact, body axis, and breath provide data that choreography memory cannot replace.

5. Closure and responsibility for outcome

Many practitioners lose quality at the end: after the throw they switch off awareness, or lose distance control. Fifth principle is zanshin and responsibility. A technique ends only when the situation is truly stable and safe—for both sides.

What absence of these principles looks like

  • Too much force: the technique works only on weaker partners and collapses under resistance.
  • No structure: movement is fast but directionless and inconsistent in closure.
  • Repeat mistakes: the student knows names, but repeats the same technical gaps every week.
  • Selective listening: feedback is accepted only when it does not require changing a habit.
  • Demonstration over quality: technique is performed to look good, not to follow principle.

Seminar example: same shape, different quality

At seminars with a guest instructor, we often see the same pattern. A technique appears "familiar," but entry timing and direction emphasis differ from daily habit. Open practitioners re-check all five principles and adapt quickly. Closed practitioners keep reproducing old habits under new commentary and stall their development.

How to train the five principles consciously

  • One principle per round: focus one series on one criterion (e.g., entry only) instead of fixing everything at once.
  • Short correction loop: after feedback, do 2–3 immediate repetitions with one concrete change.
  • Partner rotation: test principles on different bodies and tempos to separate technique from familiarity.
  • Name error precisely: not "it failed," but "my entry was late" or "I lost direction after kuzushi."
  • Finish every technique: zanshin, distance, and readiness for continuation are part of execution, not extras.

Conclusion

Different techniques teach different forms, but progress rests on the same foundation. These five principles—entry, balance, direction, contact, and closure—are a quality filter that works at every level. If you train through them consistently, technique becomes simpler, stronger, and more honest.
