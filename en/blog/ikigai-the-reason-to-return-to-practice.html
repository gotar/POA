Aikido / Budō

Ikigai (生き甲斐)

The meaning of practice rarely arrives as a revelation. More often, it is the reason to return to the mat.

May 22, 2026

The training bag is by the door. The keikogi is not quite dry. The day has been long, the body is not asking for effort, and the mind already has a list of reasons why skipping today would be reasonable. Nothing dramatic. Just an ordinary evening in which training can lose to comfort before you even leave home.

That is exactly when you find out whether practice has roots deeper than mood. This is not about making a grand declaration that Aikido is the meaning of life. Such sentences are too large, and often too easy. It is about something quieter: the reason why, despite fatigue, you tie the belt, bow, and make the first repetition honestly.

This is a useful place to speak about Ikigai (生き甲斐). The word is often translated as “a reason for living,” “what makes life worth living,” or “the reason you get up in the morning.” It combines iki — life, being alive — with kai/gai, meaning worth, value, or what is worth the effort. In the dojo, Ikigai does not have to be a romantic answer to the question of destiny. It can be much more concrete: why you return to practice when there are no fireworks.

Not a grand goal, but a reason that survives an ordinary week

Western articles often present Ikigai as a four-circle diagram: what you love, what you are good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for. That model can be useful for discussing work, but it easily turns Ikigai into a career project. In Japanese use, the concept is broader and more modest. It can concern family, a garden, friends, craft, daily responsibility, a hobby, or a small activity that gives the day direction.

This matters for practice. If Ikigai means only a great passion, most training sessions stop counting. Ordinary training is not always passionate. Sometimes it is repetition of basics. Sometimes it is repairing the same error again. Sometimes it is calmly moving through a period in which the body learns more slowly than the ego wants to admit.

That is why Ikigai works better as a question about a durable reason than about temporary excitement. What makes you return when progress is not spectacular? What makes you care about the bow, the quality of attack, the cleanliness of basics, and attention to the partner when no one rewards you for it? What is worth doing simple things more honestly for years?

Motivation is a spark, but it is not the fire

Beginners often start with motivation. A new art, a new uniform, new words, a new body in motion. Everything tastes like discovery. That stage is necessary, but it cannot be the foundation. Motivation depends on novelty. Budō practice sooner or later removes novelty and leaves repetition.

That is not a defect. It is a cleaning mechanism. When the freshness disappears, the question remains: are you training only because practice gives you the pleasant feeling of development, or because you understand its value even when it is hard, monotonous, or inconvenient? Ikigai does not have to be an emotion. More often, it is a quiet agreement that something is worth regular effort.

On the mat this is very simple to see. One person waits for an exceptional class, an exceptional partner, and an exceptional mood. Another takes an ordinary exercise and finds material for work inside it. Not because they are having a better day. Because they know why they came. The difference is not mood. The difference is the rootedness of practice.

Meaning passes through the body, not through a slogan

Ikigai is easy to say. It is harder to show in the body. If someone's reason for practice is growth, it has to be visible in how they receive correction. If it is service to the dojo, it has to be visible in how they work with a beginner. If it is inner order, it has to be visible in breath, posture, and the way the technique is finished.

Budō has the advantage of not allowing beautiful words to hide for long. A declaration quickly meets a partner, fatigue, fear, ambition, and resistance. Someone who says they seek calm but stiffens defensively at every correction has received information. Someone who says they want to serve the group but trains carelessly with beginners has received information. Someone who says they love the Path but cares only about techniques that look good has also received information.

In this sense, Ikigai is not decoration for practice. It is tested by practice. It does not only ask what moves you. It asks whether what moves you can become repeatable behavior: showing up, listening, correcting, helping, working on basics, and remaining willing to be a student long after the pleasure of being new has disappeared.

A reason that includes others

In research and popular descriptions of Ikigai, one theme returns often: connection with family, friends, community, elders who remain active, and people who feel that their presence matters to others. This does not mean that Ikigai must be social in a simple way. But in a dojo, it is difficult to separate one's own meaning from the people with whom one trains.

Practice only for oneself quickly becomes small. Of course, everyone arrives with their own body, history, limitations, and needs. But every repetition happens with someone. Uke gives the attack. Tori gives the answer. The instructor gives correction. A senior student gives an example. A beginner gives an opportunity for patience and precision. The dojo is a network of dependencies, not a room full of private self-improvement projects.

Mature Ikigai in Aikido therefore does not sound like: “I train to feel exceptional.” It sounds more like: “I train because this practice orders my life and allows me to be useful to the partner, the teacher, the group, and my own development.” This is less dramatic. That is why it is stronger. It does not require constant fascination with oneself. It requires a presence that gives something to the practice.

When meaning becomes rhythm

After several years of regular training, Ikigai may stop looking like an answer and begin to look like a rhythm. A person does not analyze every time whether going to the mat is worth it. They go because that decision has already been tested many times. This is not blind automation. It is trust in a process that has repeatedly proved larger than a temporary mood.

Such rhythm is not an escape from questions. On the contrary, it allows better questions. Why am I training now, when the body is different than it was five years ago? How should I practice so that I do not use a partner to feed ambition? What can I give the group when I am no longer just receiving knowledge? How do I preserve Shoshin when experience begins to tempt me into routine?

Ikigai does not remove such questions. It gives them weight. If practice is genuinely one of the reasons life has direction, it cannot be treated like a hobby consumed only when convenient. It has to be cared for: through presence, honesty, rest, correction, good contact with the partner, and responsibility for what one brings into the dojo.

Beyond the dojo: meaning without large slogans

Beyond the mat, Ikigai helps separate meaning from special effects. Not everything important will be exciting. Caring for family, patient craft, honest work, looking after health, teaching someone the basics, building a relationship, and returning to a difficult conversation rarely look like great discoveries. Often, they look like repetition.

The dojo teaches that repetition does not have to kill meaning. It can protect it. If you know why you return to basics, it becomes easier to return to things outside the dojo that do not give immediate reward but build a life: conversation instead of avoidance, work done properly instead of merely impressively, rest before the body forces a stop, presence with people who are too easy to treat as obvious.

Ikigai is not a magical answer to a crisis of meaning. It is more demanding. It asks what in your life is worth daily care even when no one applauds. It asks what reason can survive fatigue, repetition, and lack of immediate results. It asks whether you can live so that value does not remain only in the head, but passes into the rhythm of the day.

The bag by the door does not solve life. But sometimes it tells the truth faster than big plans. If it is packed, if you leave despite ordinary resistance, if after entering the mat you make the first repetition with attention — meaning is no longer an abstraction. It has the weight of a keikogi, the smell of the mat, the breath in the bow, and a simple decision: I return because this builds me and lets me serve what matters with more care.
