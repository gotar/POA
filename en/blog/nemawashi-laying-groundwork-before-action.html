Aikido / Budō

Nemawashi (根回し)

What looks like a smooth change usually begins much earlier — beneath the surface.

May 16, 2026

The most important part of a technique often happens before anyone sees it. Before the hand moves, distance is already being set. Before you enter, you are reading the partner's tension. Before the throw appears, the body has already decided whether it will meet the attack in the right place or fall back on strength because the preparation came too late.

Nemawashi (根回し) literally means working “around the roots.” In gardening, it referred to preparing the roots of a tree before transplanting it so the change of place would not kill the plant. In social and organizational life, the word came to mean quietly laying the groundwork: conversations, reading resistance, clarifying intention, and building agreement before a decision is presented publicly.

In the dojo, this idea is very concrete. A good technique, a good seminar, and healthy group development rarely begin with a loud announcement. They begin earlier: in the way the body is positioned, in the quality of relationships, in preparing people for change before change becomes a requirement.

Movement that already has ground

Beginners often see only the moment of action: entry, turn, lock, throw. A more experienced practitioner knows that if these things have to “save” the technique, the work began too late. Ma-ai, foot placement, the line of the shoulders, the way a grip is received, and the first contact with the partner's center are not decoration. They are preparation of the roots.

When that preparation is missing, technique becomes an emergency repair project. Tori tries to compensate for bad distance with arm strength. The turn tries to mask the delay. Kuzushi is expected to appear only after the partner is already stable and asking with the body: “all right, what now?” That is not flow. It is improvisation after neglect.

Nemawashi in technique does not mean hesitation. It is not about preparing for a long time before finally doing something. It is about giving movement the conditions in which it can exist. A good entry sometimes looks sudden precisely because the work was done beforehand: distance was right, breath held steady, contact was not empty, and decision did not have to force its way through chaos.

Learning from others: preparing for a different logic

This phenomenon is most clearly seen when someone from outside enters the mat — a teacher from another school or someone who interprets techniques in a different way. On the surface, the situation is simple: someone presents new or familiar material in a different form, and the group practices it. True learning, however, begins before anyone even steps onto the mat.

Without preparation and a clear sense of purpose for such a meeting, it is easy to waste time defending old habits. Practitioners start comparing, analyzing, and instinctively correcting the new transmission, trying to force it into their own patterns. Since a technique looks familiar, the body naturally tends to do it "its own way". This prevents genuine absorption of a different perspective.

Good Nemawashi in this context is not about extensive announcements or creating the right atmosphere. It is about working on the group's open attitude: the awareness that someone will present a different body organization, different timing, and a different way of building contact. The point is not a quick evaluation of usefulness, but a genuine engagement with the logic of a different movement, to fully experience it and be able to draw from it.

It is the senior students who build this ground with their attitude. If they react to new instructions with openness, younger students also learn that they do not have to defend their current way of practicing at all costs. Full commitment and attention, rather than merely "testing" the new approach, are a strong signal for the entire group. An example set by a sempai can shape openness much more effectively than any words.

Change does not take root by command

In the dojo, many changes look from the outside like a single decision: a new training order, a stronger emphasis on basics, a different way of working with beginners, a higher standard of punctuality, a clearer demand placed on senior ranks. It is easy to say, “from today, we do it this way.” Sometimes that is necessary. But if it is the only step, change often remains a slogan.

People can follow an instruction and still not change their way of thinking. They can nod and, two weeks later, return to the old rhythm. Not because they have bad intentions. Because the roots were pulled without preparation. They do not know why the change is needed, what exactly it concerns, what they must let go of, and what must be maintained without negotiation.

Nemawashi in leading a group is the work before the visible decision. Speaking with the people who will carry the change. Checking where resistance will appear. Explaining the standard before it becomes a public demand. Hearing reasonable objections, because sometimes resistance is not laziness but information that the plan has a blind spot.

This does not weaken the decision. It strengthens it. A decision made after such preparation does not hang in the air. It has people who understand its purpose and relationships strong enough to weather the first friction. Then change is not a tree being moved by its leaves. The roots have been prepared.

Quiet conversations without politics

Nemawashi is easily misunderstood as behind-the-scenes maneuvering. Then it becomes manipulation: before the decision is announced, someone has already assigned roles, neutralized opposition, and created pressure so no one can say “no.” That is not Nemawashi. That is politics in softer packaging.

Honest groundwork has a different quality. It does not remove people's voice; it gives that voice room to be heard before the situation becomes public and rigid. A private conversation lets someone ask a simple question without losing face. It lets the instructor see that the problem is not bad faith, but a lack of understanding. It lets a senior student accept responsibility before the group starts watching.

The same principle works in correction. Some comments can be given publicly because they serve everyone. Others are better given quietly, before a person shuts down. Not out of softness, but out of precision. The goal is not to win the scene, but to bring correction to the place where it can actually be received.

The roots beneath visible movement

Nemawashi teaches patience, but not passivity. It is not postponing action until everyone is comfortably ready. In budō, readiness is rarely complete. The demand is harder than that: responsibility for the conditions in which action will happen.

Before technique, that means distance, contact, breath, and decision set before force starts fighting force. Before facing new challenges on the mat, it means a group prepared to learn rather than compare. Before a change in the dojo, it means people who understand why the standard must rise and what part of the work they are taking on.

Visible movement is only the last layer. If there is no ground underneath, it may look impressive for a moment and fall apart at the first resistance. If the ground is prepared, the movement can be simple, even quiet — and effective precisely because of that. A tree does not stand because of its leaves. It stands because of what is usually unseen.
