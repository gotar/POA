Aikido / Budō

Nemawashi (根回し)

What looks like smooth change usually began much earlier — beneath the surface.

May 16, 2026

The most important part of a technique often happens before anyone sees the technique. Before the hand moves, distance is already being set. Before you enter, you are reading the partner's tension. Before the throw appears, the body has already decided whether it will meet the attack in the right place or rescue itself with strength because the preparation came too late.

Nemawashi (根回し) literally means working “around the roots.” In gardening, it referred to preparing the roots of a tree before transplanting it so the change of place would not kill the plant. In social and organizational life, the word came to mean quietly laying the groundwork: conversations, reading resistance, clarifying intention, and building agreement before a decision is presented publicly.

In the dojo, this idea is very concrete. A good technique, a good seminar, and healthy group development rarely begin with a loud announcement. They begin earlier: in the way the body is positioned, in the quality of relationships, in preparing people for change before change becomes a requirement.

Movement that already has ground

Beginners often see only the moment of action: entry, turn, lock, throw. A more experienced practitioner knows that if these things have to “save” the technique, the work began too late. Ma-ai, foot placement, the line of the shoulders, the way a grip is received, and the first contact with the partner's center are not decoration. They are preparation of the roots.

When that preparation is missing, technique becomes an emergency repair project. Tori tries to compensate for bad distance with arm strength. The turn tries to cover delay. Kuzushi is expected to appear only after the partner is already stable and asking with the body: “all right, what now?” That is not flow. It is improvisation after neglect.

Nemawashi in technique does not mean hesitation. It is not about preparing for a long time before finally doing something. It is about giving movement the conditions in which it can exist. A good entry sometimes looks sudden precisely because the work was done beforehand: distance was right, breath did not escape, contact was not empty, and decision did not have to force its way through chaos.

A seminar begins before the first bow

Nemawashi is especially visible during a seminar with a teacher from another school or another line of interpretation. On paper the matter is simple: an instructor arrives, shows material, and the group practices. In reality, the seminar begins much earlier.

If the group does not know why the teacher is coming, what to listen for, and how to receive a different logic of movement, the first hour will be wasted defending old habits. People will compare, filter, and correct someone else's transmission through their own method. The technique will have a familiar name, so the body will immediately try to do it “our way.” And exactly there, learning closes.

Good preparation is not advertising the guest or creating a festive atmosphere. It means setting the group's intention: someone is coming to show familiar material through a different organization of body, timing, and contact. Our task is not to judge immediately whether it fits our habits. Our task is to enter an unfamiliar logic honestly enough to actually feel it for a while.

Senior students do more here than posters and registrations. Their posture prepares the ground. If they receive correction openly, newer students see that they do not need to defend their own version of the movement. If they train with full attention instead of merely “testing” the teacher, the group receives a signal: here, we are really learning. One good act by a sempai can do more for a seminar than ten organizational sentences.

Change does not take root by command

In the dojo, many changes look from the outside like a single decision: a new training order, a stronger emphasis on basics, a different way of working with beginners, a higher standard of punctuality, a clearer demand placed on senior ranks. It is easy to say, “from today, we do it this way.” Sometimes that is necessary. But if it is the only step, change often remains a slogan.

People can follow an instruction and still not change their way of thinking. They can nod and, two weeks later, return to the old rhythm. Not because they have bad intentions. Because the roots were pulled without preparation. They do not know why the change is needed, what exactly it concerns, what they must let go of, and what must be maintained without negotiation.

Nemawashi in leading a group is the work before the visible decision. Speaking with the people who will carry the change. Checking where resistance will appear. Explaining the standard before it becomes a public demand. Hearing reasonable objections, because sometimes resistance is not laziness but information that the plan has a blind spot.

This does not weaken the decision. It strengthens it. A decision made after such preparation does not hang in the air. It has people who understand its purpose and relationships capable of carrying the first friction. Then change is not a tree being moved by its leaves. The roots have been prepared.

Quiet conversations without politics

Nemawashi is easily misunderstood as backstage arranging. Then it becomes manipulation: before the decision is announced, someone has already assigned roles, neutralized opposition, and created pressure so no one can say “no.” That is not Nemawashi. That is politics in softer packaging.

Honest groundwork has a different quality. It does not remove people's voice; it gives that voice somewhere to appear before the situation becomes public and rigid. A private conversation lets someone ask a simple question without losing face. It lets the instructor see that the problem is not bad will, but lack of understanding. It lets a senior student accept responsibility before the group starts watching.

The same principle works in correction. Some comments can be given publicly because they serve everyone. Others are better given quietly, before a person closes into defense. Not out of softness, but out of precision. The goal is not to win the scene, but to bring correction to the place where it can actually be received.

The roots beneath visible movement

Nemawashi teaches patience, but not passive patience. It is not postponing action until everyone is comfortably ready. In budō, readiness is rarely complete. The issue is harder than that: responsibility for the conditions in which action will happen.

Before technique, that means distance, contact, breath, and decision set before force starts fighting force. Before a seminar, it means a group prepared to learn rather than compare. Before a change in the dojo, it means people who understand why the standard must rise and what part of the work they are taking on.

Visible movement is only the last layer. If there is no ground underneath, it may look impressive for a moment and fall apart at the first resistance. If the ground is prepared, the movement can be simple, even quiet — and effective precisely because of that. A tree does not stand because of its leaves. It stands because of what is usually unseen.
