Aikido / Budō

The Path and Mastery

Techniques can be mastered. The principles behind them have to be discovered.

May 18, 2026

After several years of regular practice, something shifts. A practitioner has learned essentially everything the school offers. The techniques, the entries, the angles, the timing. The form can be reproduced — cleanly, calmly, without obvious mistakes.

Then one day, during an ordinary training session, a technique fails. Not because it was executed badly. It looks exactly as it should. But the partner doesn't yield. Something doesn't work, and there is no clear reason why.

This is one of the more important thresholds on the path. Not the one where a practitioner discovers how much they already know. The one where they realize that for a long time, they confused knowing the form with understanding it.

Technique is a gate, not a home

Techniques are necessary. Without them, there is no beginning of the path. But they are a tool, not a destination. Behind every form lies something that isn't immediately visible: direction of force, quality of contact, the right moment to enter, the ability to read a partner. These are principles. And mastering a technique does not mean understanding them.

This becomes clear when working with different partners. A technique may work against someone smaller, slower, or more compliant — and fail when it meets genuine resistance, varied timing, or an unexpected angle of attack. That means one thing: the form was mastered, but the principle behind it was not yet discovered.

A master is someone who has stopped collecting techniques. Instead, they take them apart and ask what makes them work at all. They are not looking for more forms. They are looking for what lies beneath the forms they already know.

When "I know everything" becomes "I know nothing"

There is a paradox in the budō tradition that can easily sound like a spiritual pose: the master says they know nothing. From the outside, it looks like false modesty. But it is neither a pose nor modesty.

A beginner doesn't know because they lack the tools. The forms, the vocabulary, the feel — none of it is there yet. A master also doesn't know — but differently. They have the form, but they see clearly how many principles behind it they still don't fully understand. They know the path, but they see how far it still extends. Their "I know nothing" is a precise observation, not an expression of humility.

This is the space where a master's real work begins. Not in accumulating more techniques, but in discovering the principles that give those techniques life. That work has no endpoint. And that is exactly what separates it from simply knowing the repertoire.

The stability that allows openness

André Cognard, an aikido master, wrote:

A master cannot be swayed in any way, while at the same time being genuinely open to the student's actions and truly listening to their words. He teaches with gentleness, without haste, without anger. He places the other above himself.

— André Cognard, Living Without an Enemy

This passage describes something harder than technique: the capacity to be open without losing one's own center.

An immature person often faces a false choice. Either close off and defend — to avoid being hurt, destabilized, changed against their will. Or open up completely — and lose their footing, surrender to other people's expectations, emotions, or pressure. A master does something else: they are genuinely open precisely because they are grounded enough that they don't need to defend themselves.

That stability is not hardness. It is not indifference. It is the result of long work — work in which body and mind have learned to return to center even under pressure, through fatigue, and in the face of the unexpected. A master can listen to a student not out of politeness, but because there is nothing to defend. They can place the other above themselves because their own center is no longer threatened by what the student brings.

The same applies to anger. An instructor who corrects from anger is usually reacting to their own helplessness, not to the student's mistake. A master who teaches without anger is not indifferent to error. They see it more clearly. But they correct from a place of calm — because calm is a condition of precision.

The same process unfolds outside the dojo

The dojo is a place where things are visible because they are concentrated. Pressure, resistance, ego, fear — everything arrives faster and more sharply there than it does in ordinary life. But what a person learns on the mat doesn't stop at the dojo door. If it does, it was never truly understood.

The principles a master discovers work outside the training hall exactly as they do inside it. Distance — not only in combat, but in conversation, conflict, and decision-making. Timing — not only in entering a technique, but in knowing when to speak, when to stay silent, when to act. Balance — not only a physical position, but an internal state one can return to. Openness without losing center — not only in the master-student relationship, but in any relationship where something genuinely important is at stake.

This becomes visible in professional work, parenting, leadership. Someone who knows the procedures but doesn't understand the principles behind them will be helpless in any situation the procedures don't anticipate. Someone who has learned the forms of communication but doesn't understand what genuinely serves the other person will use the right words in the wrong way.

The Path is exactly this: moving from knowing forms to understanding the principles behind them. And that process has no point at which one can say: done. Mastery is not a destination. It is an attitude toward practice — a persistent question about what lies beneath the form. It is carried in the whole body, across an entire life — not only on the mat.
